Strengthening Entrepreneurial Ecosystems for Small Business Growth in Mexico's Intermediate Cities · Fundación Coppel · ANDE

Theory of Change · Full Causal Chain

If the organizations that support entrepreneurs become stronger, the actors of each city co-create the solution to their ecosystem's challenges, and micro-businesses receive practical training, new capabilities, and a first productive capital, then the ecosystem converts investment into growing businesses — because local ownership and verifiable information reduce the cost and the risk of investing in it.

Beneficiaries: entrepreneurs and micro-businesses in Mexico's intermediate cities and, through them, their families and communities. Entrepreneur support organizations (ESOs) and ecosystem actors are intermediate beneficiaries.

Three cross-cutting objectives run through every intervention and provide the shared framework for measuring impact at the entrepreneur, institutional, and ecosystem levels: business formalization, digitalization, and access to financial products and services. Inputs
Philanthropic capitalFundación Coppel contributes USD $1,636,000 and retains the decision over its allocation; Visa Foundation contributes USD $1,000,000 (36-month request). This is the catalytic capital that activates the ESO calls, the Action Labs, and the seed capital.
Public co-investmentLocal (state or municipal) governments match the seed capital 1:1: USD $660,000 over the three years, under annual agreements.
Local ecosystem counterpartParticipating organizations and their allies (universities, governments, companies) contribute 1:1 counterpart funding to every funded project: USD $674,157 in the Component 1 calls, plus the counterpart each Action Lab mobilizes.
ANDE's method and knowledgeThe ecosystem diagnostic (seven domains, five actor types, four maturity levels), applied in ten intermediate cities as the common baseline; the MIT D-Lab participatory design methodology; the SCALE framework and initial capability assessments; and the measurement framework.
Technology platformsCoppel Emprende (free national online training, 100+ short lessons) and andeimpact.org (results reporting and traceability), with landing pages and dashboards per state.
Institutional capacity and alliancesThe Fundación Coppel and ANDE teams; FUNDES as Fundación Coppel's operating partner for Component 3; the executing NGOs in each state; and the registry of eligible organizations that Coppel and ANDE maintain.
Activities
C1 · Organizational Strengthening
Annual two-track ESO call: design, invitation, evaluation, and selectionFundación Coppel funds the grant pool and defines the priority states of each edition, which weigh in favor without excluding the rest of the country. Organizations in the Coppel–ANDE registry are invited, and a committee evaluates blind, with calibration interviews, to select about ten organizations per edition — three editions (2026–2028), grants of up to half the project cost with 1:1 counterpart.
Project implementation · Track 1: Entrepreneurship programsThe organization delivers its training program: at least three elements among business skills, mentoring, specialized training, financial modeling, financing, market access, and social capital; minimum three months; at least 50 entrepreneurs; entry and exit assessment. It may include seed capital for participants (up to 50% of cost, preferably in kind). Up to MXN $500,000 (~USD $28,000) per project, implemented in twelve months.
Project implementation · Track 2: Organizational strengtheningThe organization runs a project to modernize, update, strengthen, or measure its existing entrepreneur-training model, with training, technical assistance, and accompaniment. The project prepares the model for potential replication in earlier-stage ecosystems. Up to MXN $450,000 (~USD $25,000) per project.
ANDE accompaniment and measurement on andeimpact.orgANDE assesses each organization's starting point and accompanies it throughout implementation with the SCALE framework, supporting metrics, fundraising, governance, institutionalization, and communications. Each organization reports on andeimpact.org at mid-term, at close, and four months after closing; the platform traces supported businesses, including their formalization status.
C2 · Ecosystem Development (Action Labs)
Lab preparation and diagnostic socializationA local advisory committee (3–6 city referents) frames the challenge from the diagnostic and helps recruit, by invitation, 4 to 10 organizations of the ecosystem. In the first in-person meeting ANDE presents the diagnostic findings; the actors recognize themselves in their own data and build a shared vision of the challenge.
Facilitation of the participatory design cycleANDE facilitates about nine sessions with the MIT D-Lab methodology, in five phases — learn, imagine, create, test, synthesize — over six to twelve months. The real work happens between sessions, inside the participating organizations. Twelve labs over the three years, four per year.
Peer selection and funding of the winning solutionTeams present their solutions and the participants themselves select the most promising one with a predefined rubric; allied funders can be invited to the committee. To qualify, every solution must directly address an ecosystem challenge while advancing at least one of the three cross-cutting objectives: digitalization, formalization, or access to financial products. The MXN $1,000,000 (~USD $56,000), secured from the start, is delivered at the end to the winning group through its authorized nonprofit or a host nonprofit of the ecosystem, activating the 1:1 counterpart.
Solution implementation and cycle documentationThe winning group implements the solution (for example, an angel investor club or an inter-institutional collaboration mechanism). Results are measured against the diagnostic baseline and each city produces a learning brief that feeds the others.
C3 · Business Capability Development & Access to Capital
Continuous operation of the Coppel Emprende platformFree, online, nationwide training open to any micro-business that registers: 100+ short lessons on management, digitalization, and payment methods, live webinars, community, and progress incentives. Runs continuously for the 36 months, with a target of 21,000 micro-entrepreneurs enrolled (6,000 / 7,000 / 8,000 per year).
Development of the Visa-aligned learning module (30 lessons)A dedicated learning pathway on the three cross-cutting objectives: 30 lessons over the grant — 15 new and 15 updated (5 + 5 per year). Topics are defined each January from the platform's annual entrepreneur feedback cycle; development takes 5–6 months and each cohort of lessons launches in June. Completion is certified automatically by the platform. The new lessons and modules offer a co-branding opportunity for Visa Foundation.
State co-investment agreements, matched 1:1Each state government submits its project, signs the agreement, commits the budget matched 1:1 by Fundación Coppel (up to MXN $500,000 / ~USD $28,000 per cohort from Coppel), and designates the executing NGO. The NGO registers in the partner portal and builds the cohort budget with the government and the Foundation before the call opens. 18 state cohorts over the three years, with annual calls.
State calls, selection, and delivery of in-kind seed capitalThe NGO publishes the state call and selects, with a standardized needs assessment, among operating micro-businesses (1–10 workers) that completed the first training level (41 lessons). Capital is delivered in kind — equipment, tools, and inputs purchased from formal suppliers — up to MXN $8,000 (~USD $450) per business, with a minimum of 100 micro-businesses per cohort.
Verification, follow-up, and technical enablementThe NGO verifies delivery with a final report in the partner portal (beneficiary database, assessments, amounts, and support items); up to 35% of Coppel's resources may fund its operation. Fundación Coppel and its operating partner provide periodic follow-up and enable a Coppel Emprende landing page and dashboard per state.
Cross-component
Conduct and update the ecosystem diagnosticANDE applies the diagnostic model (seven domains, five actor types, four maturity levels), the common baseline of the three components. It is already complete in ten intermediate cities and extends to new cities as they mature.
Administer grants and verify counterpartsSign the agreements of each component, disburse the resources, value in-kind contributions, and verify the 1:1 counterpart of every project. Fundación Coppel contributes the capital and retains the allocation decision.
Measure and report the full strategyANDE consolidates the indicators of the three components into a single table, disaggregates businesses and jobs by sex, and produces the mid-term (Year 2) and final (Year 3) measurement reports.
Re-diagnose, publish learning, and hand overAt close, ANDE re-diagnoses each city to measure its maturity gain, publishes the learning briefs, and hands the work over to the local actors, who remain its owners.
